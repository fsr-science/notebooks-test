# 📖 CHAPTER 1 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY
> **Some Basic Concepts of Chemistry** | All terms alphabetically arranged with precise scientific definitions and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

> [!info] **Accuracy** *(Board/NEET)*
> The degree to which a measured value agrees with the **true or accepted value** of the quantity. A measurement can be precise without being accurate, but a truly accurate measurement must be precise.
>
> *Contrast with: Precision (closeness of repeated measurements to each other)*

---

> [!note] **Alchemy**
> A pre-scientific tradition that sought to convert base metals into gold and find the elixir of life. Chemistry developed from alchemical traditions (1300–1600 CE). In India, it existed alongside Iatrochemistry.

---

> [!note] **Atom**
> The smallest indivisible particle of an element that retains the chemical properties of that element. Proposed as 'Paramānu' by Acharya Kanda (600 BCE) and formally defined in Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808). Atoms may exist singly (Na, Cu) or as molecules (H₂, O₂).

---

> [!note] **Atomic Mass** *(Board/NEET)*
> The mass of an atom expressed in **atomic mass units (u)**. Currently referenced to ¹²C = 12 u exactly (standard adopted in 1961). In practice, the **average atomic mass** is used, which accounts for isotopic abundances.

---

> [!note] **Atomic Mass Unit (amu / u)** *(All levels)*
> A unit of mass equal to **1/12 the mass of one ¹²C atom**.
>
> $$1 \text{ u} = 1 \text{ amu} = 1.66056 \times 10^{-24} \text{ g}$$
>
> The modern symbol is **u** (unified mass unit).

---

> [!important] **Average Atomic Mass** *(NEET/JEE)*
> A weighted average of the masses of all naturally occurring isotopes of an element, weighted by their relative abundances.
>
> $$\bar{A} = \sum_i (\text{fractional abundance}_i \times \text{atomic mass}_i)$$

---

> [!important] **Avogadro's Constant (Nₐ)** *(All levels)*
> The number of elementary entities in **one mole** of a substance.
>
> $$N_A = 6.02214076 \times 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{-1}$$
>
> Named in honour of Amedeo Avogadro.

---

> [!note] **Avogadro's Law** *(Board/NEET)*
> Proposed in 1811: "Equal volumes of all gases at the same temperature and pressure contain equal numbers of molecules." Distinguished between atoms and molecules.

---

## B

> [!note] **Balanced Chemical Equation**
> A chemical equation in which the number of atoms of each element is the same on both the reactant and product sides, satisfying the Law of Conservation of Mass. Only **coefficients** (not subscripts) are changed to balance.

---

> [!note] **Bhasma**
> Metal preparations described in *Charaka Samhita* used in Ayurvedic medicine. Modern studies show bhasmas contain **nanoparticles** of metals — an ancient application of what is now called nanotechnology.

---

## C

> [!note] **Candela (cd)** *(Board)*
> The SI base unit of **luminous intensity**. Defined by the luminous efficacy of monochromatic radiation of frequency 540 × 10¹² Hz.

---

> [!note] **Chemical Property**
> A property that can only be observed when a substance undergoes a **chemical change** (i.e., the substance is transformed into a different substance). Examples: combustibility, acidity, reactivity with acids.

---

> [!note] **Compound** *(All levels)*
> A pure substance formed when two or more elements combine chemically in a **fixed and definite ratio by mass**. Properties of a compound are different from those of its constituent elements. Components can only be separated by chemical methods.

---

> [!note] **Concentration**
> The amount of solute present in a given quantity of solution or solvent. Expressed as molarity, molality, mole fraction, or mass per cent.

---

## D

> [!important] **Dalton's Atomic Theory (1808)** *(All levels)*
> Published in *'A New System of Chemical Philosophy'*. Key postulates: atoms are indivisible; atoms of the same element are identical; compounds form from fixed atom ratios; atoms are rearranged (not created/destroyed) in reactions.
>
> > ⚠️ Limitations: could not explain isotopes, isobars, or Gay Lussac's Law.

---

> [!note] **Density** *(Board/NEET)*
> Mass per unit volume of a substance: $\rho = m/V$.
>
> | Unit system | Unit |
> |:---:|:---:|
> | SI | kg m⁻³ |
> | Lab | g cm⁻³ or g mL⁻¹ |
>
> Higher density = more closely packed particles.

---

> [!note] **Dimensional Analysis** *(All levels)*
> Also called **factor label method** or **unit factor method**. A mathematical technique for unit conversion that involves multiplying a quantity by **unit factors** (ratios equal to 1) to cancel unwanted units and introduce desired units.

---

## E

> [!note] **Element** *(All levels)*
> A pure substance consisting of only **one type of atom**. Cannot be broken down into simpler substances by chemical means. May exist as single atoms (Na, Fe) or molecules (H₂, O₂, P₄, S₈).

---

> [!important] **Empirical Formula** *(NEET/JEE)*
> A chemical formula that shows the **simplest whole number ratio** of atoms of each element present in a compound. Derived from experimental % composition data. May or may not be same as molecular formula.
>
> Example: CH₂O is the empirical formula for both formaldehyde (CH₂O) and glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆).

---

> [!note] **Elixir of Life**
> A mythical substance that alchemists sought, believed to grant immortality. Along with the Philosopher's Stone, it was a primary motivation for early chemical investigation.

---

## F

> [!note] **Faience**
> A type of glass made by the Harappan civilisation, used in ornaments. Evidence of early glass-making in ancient India.

---

> [!important] **Formula Mass** *(Board/NEET)*
> Used in place of molecular mass for **ionic compounds** (like NaCl) that do not exist as discrete molecules but as three-dimensional lattice structures. Calculated as the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in the formula unit.
>
> Example: NaCl = 23 + 35.5 = **58.5 u**

---

## G

> [!important] **Gay Lussac's Law of Gaseous Volumes (1808)** *(Board/NEET)*
> "When gases combine or are produced in a chemical reaction, they do so in a **simple ratio by volume**, provided all gases are at the same temperature and pressure." Correctly explained by Avogadro's Law.

---

## H

> [!note] **Heterogeneous Mixture** *(Board)*
> A mixture in which the composition is **not uniform** throughout. Different components may be visible. Example: soil, sand + salt, oil + water.

---

> [!note] **Homogeneous Mixture** *(Board)*
> A mixture in which the composition is **uniform throughout**. Also called a **solution**. Example: air, sugar solution, vinegar.

---

## I

> [!note] **Iatrochemistry**
> A branch of chemistry (1300–1600 CE) focused on the application of chemistry to medicine. Preceded modern chemistry.

---

> [!important] **Isotopes** *(NEET/JEE)*
> Atoms of the **same element** (same atomic number) but with **different mass numbers** (different numbers of neutrons).
>
> Example: ¹²C, ¹³C, ¹⁴C are all isotopes of carbon.
>
> > ⚠️ The existence of isotopes is a limitation of Dalton's Atomic Theory.

---

## K

> [!note] **Kelvin (K)** *(Board)*
> The SI base unit of **thermodynamic temperature**. Defined using the Boltzmann constant.
>
> $$K = °C + 273.15$$
>
> The kelvin scale has no negative values (absolute zero = 0 K = −273.15°C).

---

> [!note] **Kilogram (kg)** *(Board/All)*
> The SI base unit of **mass**. Defined by the fixed value of Planck's constant $h = 6.62607015 \times 10^{-34}$ J·s.

---

## L

> [!important] **Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier, 1789)** *(All levels)*
> "In all physical and chemical changes, there is no net change in mass during the process. Matter can neither be created nor destroyed." This forms the basis for balancing chemical equations.

---

> [!important] **Law of Definite Proportions (Proust)** *(All levels)*
> "A given compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by weight (mass)." Also called Law of Definite Composition. Confirmed by Proust's experiments on cupric carbonate.

---

> [!important] **Law of Multiple Proportions (Dalton, 1803)** *(NEET/JEE)*
> "If two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in a ratio of small whole numbers."
>
> Example: CO and CO₂ — oxygen masses 16 g and 32 g for same mass of carbon → ratio 1:2.

---

> [!important] **Limiting Reagent (Limiting Reactant)** *(NEET/JEE — most tested)*
> The reactant that is **completely consumed first** in a reaction, thereby limiting the total amount of product that can be formed. Identified by dividing moles of each reactant by its stoichiometric coefficient — smallest value indicates limiting reagent.

---

## M

> [!note] **Matter** *(All levels)*
> Anything that has **mass** and **occupies space** (has volume). Classified into solids, liquids, and gases (by state) or elements, compounds, and mixtures (by composition).

---

> [!note] **Metre (m)** *(Board)*
> The SI base unit of **length**. Defined as the length of path travelled by light in vacuum in 1/299,792,458 of a second.

---

> [!note] **Mixture** *(All levels)*
> A substance containing **two or more pure substances** combined in any ratio, with variable composition. Properties are intermediate between those of components. Separated by physical methods.

---

> [!note] **Molality (m)** *(NEET/JEE)*
> Number of moles of solute dissolved per **kilogram of solvent**. Unit: mol kg⁻¹. **Temperature-independent** (since mass doesn't change with temperature). Preferred for colligative property calculations.
>
> $$m = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{mass of solvent in kg}}$$

---

> [!important] **Molar Mass (M)** *(All levels)*
> The mass of **one mole** of a substance, expressed in g mol⁻¹. Numerically equal to the atomic/molecular/formula mass in u.
>
> Example: molar mass of H₂O = 18.02 g mol⁻¹.

---

> [!important] **Molarity (M)** *(All levels)*
> Number of moles of solute per **litre of solution**. Unit: mol L⁻¹. **Temperature-dependent** (because volume changes with temperature). Most common concentration unit in lab chemistry.
>
> $$M = \frac{\text{moles of solute}}{\text{volume of solution in litres}}$$
>
> Dilution formula: $M_1 V_1 = M_2 V_2$

---

> [!important] **Mole (mol)** *(All levels — most fundamental concept)*
> The SI base unit of **amount of substance**. One mole contains exactly $6.02214076 \times 10^{23}$ elementary entities (Avogadro's number). Can represent atoms, molecules, ions, electrons, or any other specified particles.

---

> [!note] **Molecular Formula** *(Board/NEET)*
> A chemical formula showing the **actual number** of each type of atom in one molecule of a compound. Obtained by multiplying the empirical formula by $n = \text{Molar mass} / \text{EF mass}$.

---

> [!note] **Molecular Mass** *(Board/NEET)*
> The sum of the atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule. Expressed in atomic mass units (u). Used for covalent compounds that exist as discrete molecules.

---

> [!note] **Molecule** *(All levels)*
> The smallest particle of a substance that has the chemical properties of that substance and can exist independently. May be made of atoms of the same element (H₂, O₂) or different elements (H₂O, CO₂).

---

## N

> [!note] **Nanotechnology**
> Technology dealing with structures/particles at the nanometre ($10^{-9}$ m) scale. Mentioned in context of *Charaka Samhita* — bhasmas (Ayurvedic metal preparations) are now known to contain nanoparticles.

---

## P

> [!note] **Paramānu**
> The ancient Indian term for the smallest indivisible particle of matter, proposed by **Acharya Kanda (600 BCE)**. Described as eternal, indestructible, spherical, and in motion. Comparable to the modern concept of atoms. Conceptualised ~2500 years before Dalton's atomic theory.

---

> [!note] **Philosopher's Stone (Paras)**
> A mythical substance sought by alchemists, believed to convert base metals (iron, copper) into gold. Along with the Elixir of Life, it drove early chemical investigations.

---

> [!note] **Physical Property** *(Board)*
> A property that can be measured or observed **without changing the chemical identity** of the substance. Examples: colour, density, melting point, boiling point, mass, volume.

---

> [!info] **Precision** *(Board/NEET)*
> The closeness of a set of repeated measurements to **each other** (reproducibility). Does not necessarily mean the measurements are close to the true value. A smaller least count → greater precision.
>
> *Contrast with: Accuracy (closeness to true value)*

---

> [!note] **Pure Substance** *(All levels)*
> A material with a **fixed composition** and definite, characteristic properties throughout. All constituent particles are chemically identical. Includes both elements and compounds.

---

## R

> [!note] **Rasayan Shastra**
> The ancient Indian name for chemistry (also called *Rastantra*, *Ras Kriya*, or *Rasvidya*). Encompassed metallurgy, medicine, manufacture of cosmetics, glass, and dyes.

---

> [!note] **Rasratnakar**
> A text by Nagarjuna dealing with the formulation of mercury compounds and methods for extracting metals (gold, silver, tin, copper).

---

> [!note] **Rounding Off** *(All levels)*
> The process of reducing the number of digits in a calculated result to match the precision of the measurements used.
>
> | Digit to Drop | Action |
> |:---:|:---|
> | > 5 | Raise preceding digit by 1 |
> | < 5 | Leave preceding digit unchanged |
> | = 5, preceding even | Drop (banker's rounding) |
> | = 5, preceding odd | Raise preceding digit by 1 |

---

## S

> [!note] **Scientific Notation** *(All levels)*
> A method of expressing numbers in the form $N \times 10^n$ (where $1 \leq N < 10$), used to handle very large or very small numbers conveniently and to clarify the number of significant figures.

---

> [!important] **Significant Figures** *(All levels — very important)*
> All digits in a measurement that are known with certainty **plus one final estimated (uncertain) digit**. Indicate the precision of a measurement. Rules govern which zeros are significant.

---

> [!important] **SI System (Système International d'Unités)** *(All levels)*
> The internationally agreed-upon system of measurement, established in **1960** by the **11th CGPM**. Based on **7 base units**. Replaced earlier systems like the English and Metric systems.

---

> [!important] **Stoichiometry** *(NEET/JEE)*
> *(from Greek: stoicheion = element, metron = measure)*
>
> The quantitative study of the amounts of reactants consumed and products formed in a chemical reaction, based on the balanced chemical equation.

---

> [!note] **Stoichiometric Coefficient** *(Board/NEET)*
> The numerical coefficient placed before a chemical formula in a balanced equation. Represents the number of **molecules or moles** of that species participating in the reaction.

---

## U

> [!note] **Unified Mass Unit (u)** *(All levels)*
> The modern preferred symbol for the atomic mass unit (amu). Defined as 1/12 the mass of one ¹²C atom.
>
> $$1 \text{ u} = 1.66056 \times 10^{-24} \text{ g}$$
>
> Replaces the older notation 'amu'.

---

> [!note] **Unit Factor**
> A ratio equal to **1**, used in dimensional analysis to convert between units. Both the numerator and denominator represent the same quantity in different units.
>
> Example: $\dfrac{2.54 \text{ cm}}{1 \text{ in}} = 1$

---

## V

> [!note] **Vaiseshika Sutras**
> Text authored by **Acharya Kanda (600 BCE)** containing the first formulation of the atomic theory (Paramānu theory) in India.

---

## W

> [!warning] **Weight vs. Mass** *(Board/NEET)*
> Weight = gravitational force on an object: $W = mg$
>
> | Property | Weight | Mass |
> |:---:|:---:|:---:|
> | SI unit | Newton (N) | kilogram (kg) |
> | Location-dependent? | ✅ Yes | ❌ No |
> | Measured by | Spring balance | Analytical balance |
