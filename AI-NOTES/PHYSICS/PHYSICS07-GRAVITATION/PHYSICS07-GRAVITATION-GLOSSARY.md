# CHAPTER 7 — GLOSSARY & TERMINOLOGY

### Gravitation

---

> All terms arranged **alphabetically** with precise scientific definitions, dimensional formulae where applicable, and exam relevance tags.

---

## A

**Aphelion**
The point in a planet's elliptical orbit that is **farthest** from the Sun. At the aphelion, the planet is at maximum distance $r_A$ from the Sun and moves with minimum orbital speed $v_A$. By conservation of angular momentum: $r_p v_p = r_A v_A$. For Earth, the aphelion distance ≈ 1.52 × 10¹¹ m (reached around July 4). *(All levels — critical)*

**Apogee**
The point in a satellite's orbit around Earth that is **farthest** from Earth's centre. The satellite's speed is minimum here. The term 'aphelion' is used for orbits around the Sun; 'apogee' is used for orbits around Earth. *(Board/NEET)*

---

## B

**Binding Energy (Satellite)**
The minimum energy required to remove a satellite from its orbit to infinity (i.e., to unbind it from Earth). For a circular orbit at radius $r = R_E + h$:

$$\text{Binding Energy} = |E| = \frac{GM_Em}{2(R_E + h)}$$

This equals the magnitude of the total mechanical energy of the satellite. A negative total energy implies a bound system. SI unit: J. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## C

**Cavendish Experiment**
The first experimental determination of the gravitational constant G, performed by Henry Cavendish in 1798. Uses a **torsion balance**: two small lead spheres on a bar suspended by a fine wire; two large spheres brought close create a gravitational torque that twists the wire. By measuring the twist angle θ and the known restoring couple τ per unit angle, G is calculated from $G = \tau\theta d^2/MmL$. This experiment effectively "weighed the Earth" by allowing $M_E$ to be calculated. *(Board/NEET)*

**Central Force**
A force that is always directed along the line joining the two interacting objects (along **r**). For a central force, the torque about the source = 0, so angular momentum is conserved. Gravity is a central force; this is why Kepler's second law (equal areas) holds. Any central force (not just gravity) obeys the equal-area law. *(All levels — critical)*

**Centre of Gravity (CG)**
The point at which the entire weight of a body can be assumed to act. In a **uniform gravitational field**, CG coincides with the **Centre of Mass (COM)**. For very large bodies where g varies from point to point (non-uniform g), CG ≠ COM. *(Board — conceptual)*

**Circular Orbit**
A special (limiting) case of an elliptical orbit in which the eccentricity is zero and the two foci coincide. For a circular orbit at distance r from Earth's centre: orbital speed $V = \sqrt{GM_E/r}$, time period $T = 2\pi r/V$. *(All levels — critical)*

**Copernican Model (Heliocentric)**
The model of the solar system in which the **Sun is at the centre** and the planets revolve around it. First definitively proposed by Nicolas Copernicus (1473–1543). Superseded Ptolemy's geocentric model. Copernicus assumed circular orbits; Kepler later corrected these to ellipses. *(Board — historical)*

---

## E

**Eccentricity (e)**
A measure of how much an ellipse deviates from a circle. $e = 0$ for a circle; $0 < e < 1$ for an ellipse; $e = 1$ for a parabola. For Earth's orbit $e \approx 0.0167$ (nearly circular). Defined as $e = (r_A - r_P)/(r_A + r_P) =$ distance between foci / major axis length. *(JEE)*

**Ellipse**
A closed curve for which the sum of distances from any point on the curve to the two foci (F₁ and F₂) is constant: $TF_1 + TF_2 = 2a$ (where a is the semi-major axis). Kepler's first law states all planetary orbits are ellipses. A circle is a special ellipse with $F_1 = F_2$. *(All levels)*

**Escape Speed ($v_e$)**
The minimum speed that must be given to an object at the surface of a planet so that it can escape to infinity against the planet's gravitational pull, never to return. Derived from energy conservation ($KE + PE = 0$ at minimum):

$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM_E}{R_E}} = \sqrt{2g_0R_E} \approx \textbf{11.2 km s}^{-1} \text{ for Earth}$$

Key properties: independent of the mass and the direction of projection; depends on the planet's mass and radius. SI unit: m s⁻¹. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## F

**Free Fall**
Motion of a body under the influence of gravity alone, with no other forces acting. In free fall, every part of the body has the same acceleration (g) → no internal stresses → the body is **weightless**. Orbiting satellites and their occupants are in perpetual free fall (falling toward Earth but also moving sideways fast enough to keep missing it). *(All levels — critical)*

**Focus (Foci)**
One of the two fixed points that define an ellipse. In Kepler's first law, the Sun is at one focus of each planet's elliptical orbit. The other focus is empty (no celestial body there). For a circle, both foci coincide at the centre. *(All levels)*

---

## G

**Geocentric Model**
The ancient model of the solar system proposed by Ptolemy (~150 AD) in which the **Earth is at the centre** and all other celestial objects revolve around it. Required complex epicycles (circles within circles) to explain observed retrograde planetary motions. Eventually replaced by the heliocentric model. *(Board — historical)*

**Geostationary Satellite**
An Earth satellite in a circular equatorial orbit with a time period of exactly **24 hours** (same as Earth's rotation). To an observer on Earth, it appears to remain stationary above a fixed point on the equator. Orbit height ≈ 35,800 km above the equator. Used for communications and weather observation (e.g., INSAT series). *(Board/NEET)*

**Gravitational Constant (G)**
The universal constant of proportionality in Newton's law of gravitation: $F = Gm_1m_2/r^2$. Its value is:

$$G = \textbf{6.67 × 10}^{-11}\textbf{ N m}^2\textbf{ kg}^{-2}$$

Dimensional formula: **[M⁻¹L³T⁻²]**. G is the same throughout the universe regardless of the nature of masses or the medium. First measured by Henry Cavendish (1798). *(All levels — critical)*

**Gravitational Field Intensity (g)**
The gravitational force per unit mass at a point in a gravitational field:

$$\mathbf{g} = \frac{\mathbf{F}}{m} = \frac{GM}{r^2} \quad \text{(directed toward the source mass)}$$

It is a vector. At Earth's surface, $g \approx 9.8$ m s⁻² directed toward Earth's centre. Dimensional formula: **[LT⁻²]**. *(All levels)*

**Gravitational Potential (U)**
The gravitational potential energy per unit mass at a point in a gravitational field:

$$U(r) = -\frac{GM_E}{r} \quad \text{(J kg⁻¹)}$$

Taking $V = 0$ at infinity, U is always negative. The gravitational potential is a scalar. Dimensional formula: **[L²T⁻²]**. SI unit: J kg⁻¹. *(Board/NEET)*

**Gravitational Potential Energy (V)**
The work done by an external agent in bringing a mass m from infinity to a point at distance r from the source mass, with zero kinetic energy at both ends:

$$V(r) = -\frac{GM_Em}{r}$$

This is always negative (V = 0 at $r \to \infty$). Near Earth's surface ($h \ll R_E$), $\Delta V \approx mgh$. Dimensional formula: **[ML²T⁻²]**. SI unit: J. *(All levels — critical)*

**Gravity**
The attractive force of gravitation exerted by a large body (such as Earth) on smaller masses near its surface. Gravity at Earth's surface produces the acceleration $g \approx 9.8$ m s⁻² directed toward the centre. Technically, "gravity" refers to the effect of a large body's gravitational field; "gravitational force" is the more general term. *(All levels)*

---

## H

**Heliocentric Model**
A model of the solar system in which the **Sun is at the centre** and the planets orbit around it. First mentioned by Aryabhatta; formally proposed by Copernicus (circular orbits); corrected to elliptical orbits by Kepler; explained physically by Newton. Now the accepted model. *(Board — historical)*

---

## I

**Inverse-Square Law**
The gravitational force between two masses varies as the inverse square of the distance between them: $F \propto 1/r^2$. Newton deduced this from comparing g at Earth's surface with the Moon's centripetal acceleration (ratio = 3600 = $(R_M/R_E)^2$). Many important physical forces (electrostatic, light intensity) also follow inverse-square laws. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## K

**Kepler's Laws**
Three empirical laws governing planetary motion, derived by Johannes Kepler (1571–1640) from Tycho Brahe's observations:

1. **Law of Orbits:** Planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one focus.
2. **Law of Areas:** The radius vector sweeps equal areas in equal times (conservation of angular momentum).
3. **Law of Periods:** $T^2 \propto a^3$; equivalently $T^2 = (4\pi^2/GM_S) \times a^3$.

These laws are also applicable to satellite orbits (with Earth replacing the Sun). *(All levels — critical)*

---

## N

**Newton's Universal Law of Gravitation**
Every body in the universe attracts every other body with a force that is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them:

$$|\mathbf{F}| = G\frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

In vector form: $\mathbf{F} = -G\dfrac{m_1m_2}{r^2}\hat{r}$ (attractive; along $-\hat{r}$). Forces satisfy Newton's 3rd law: $\mathbf{F}_{12} = -\mathbf{F}_{21}$. This single law unifies terrestrial gravity and celestial mechanics, and explains all of Kepler's laws. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## O

**Orbital Speed**
The speed required for a satellite to maintain a stable circular orbit at a given height h above Earth's surface:

$$V = \sqrt{\frac{GM_E}{R_E + h}}$$

For low Earth orbit ($h \approx 0$): $V_0 = \sqrt{gR_E} \approx$ **7.9 km s⁻¹** (also called the **first cosmic velocity**). Orbital speed decreases as height increases. SI unit: m s⁻¹. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## P

**Perihelion**
The point in a planet's elliptical orbit that is **closest** to the Sun. At perihelion, the planet is at minimum distance $r_p$ and moves with maximum orbital speed $v_p$. For Earth, perihelion distance ≈ 1.47 × 10¹¹ m (reached around January 3). *(All levels — critical)*

**Perigee**
The point in a satellite's orbit around Earth that is **closest** to Earth's centre. The satellite moves fastest at perigee. Analogous to perihelion for orbits around the Sun. *(Board/NEET)*

**Principle of Superposition (Gravitation)**
The total gravitational force on a mass due to multiple other masses equals the **vector sum** of the individual gravitational forces:

$$\mathbf{F}_\text{total} = \mathbf{F}_1 + \mathbf{F}_2 + \ldots + \mathbf{F}_n$$

Each force acts independently, uninfluenced by the presence of other masses. Similarly, total gravitational potential energy = algebraic sum of PE for all pairs. *(All levels — critical)*

**Ptolemaic Model**
See **Geocentric Model**. *(Board — historical)*

---

## R

**Radius of Orbit**
For a satellite in circular orbit: $r = R_E + h$, where h is the height above Earth's surface and $R_E$ is Earth's radius. This is the distance from Earth's centre to the satellite. *(All levels)*

---

## S

**Semi-Major Axis (a)**
Half the length of the major axis of an ellipse. For a planetary orbit: $a = (r_p + r_A)/2$ where $r_p$ is the perihelion distance and $r_A$ is the aphelion distance. The semi-major axis is the key quantity in Kepler's third law ($T^2 \propto a^3$). For a circular orbit, $a$ = radius of the circle. SI unit: m (or AU for astronomical distances). *(All levels — critical)*

**Shell Theorem**
Two important results for uniform spherical shells:

1. A uniform spherical shell attracts an external point mass **as if all its mass were concentrated at its centre**.
2. A uniform spherical shell exerts **zero** gravitational force on a point mass located anywhere inside it.

These results allow the Earth (treated as a set of concentric shells) to be treated as a point mass for external objects, and simplify the calculation of g at depth d. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## T

**Tidal Force**
A differential gravitational force: the difference in gravitational pull on the near and far sides of a body due to a massive object. Tidal force depends on the **gradient** of gravity ($dF/dr \propto 1/r^3$), not just F. Although the Sun exerts a larger gravitational force on Earth than the Moon, the Moon's tidal effect is greater because it is much closer (steeper gradient). *(Board — conceptual)*

**Time Period (Orbital)**
The time taken by a satellite to complete one full revolution around a central body:

$$T = \frac{2\pi(R_E + h)^{3/2}}{\sqrt{GM_E}}$$

For a low-Earth orbit ($h \approx 0$): $T_0 \approx 85$ minutes. For a geostationary orbit: T = 24 hours. T increases with orbital height. SI unit: s. *(All levels — critical)*

---

## U

**Universal Gravitation**
The concept introduced by Newton that the same force of gravity that causes objects to fall on Earth also governs the motion of planets, moons, and all celestial objects. "Universal" means the law applies everywhere in the universe, for any two masses, regardless of their nature or the medium between them. *(All levels)*

---

## V

**Virial Theorem (Gravitational)**
For a bound gravitational system in a stable circular orbit:

$$KE = -E = -\frac{1}{2} \times PE$$

Where E is total mechanical energy. This means: $|PE| = 2 \times KE$; Total energy $E = -KE = PE/2$. Always remember: for a satellite, **|PE| = 2KE** and **E is negative**. *(NEET/JEE)*

---

## W

**Weightlessness**
The condition in which an object appears to have no weight — it exerts no force on a support and a spring balance reads zero. This occurs when both the object and its support are in **free fall** (under gravity alone). An astronaut in an orbiting spacecraft is weightless because both the spacecraft and astronaut fall toward Earth together. Important: weightlessness does **NOT** mean absence of gravity. *(All levels — critical)*

## Contrast Table — Key Pairs That Students Confuse

| Aspect | Circular Orbit | Escape (to infinity) |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Energy condition | $E < 0$ (bound) | $E \geq 0$ (free) |
| Required speed | $V_0 = \sqrt{gR_E} \approx 7.9$ km s⁻¹ | $v_e = \sqrt{2gR_E} \approx 11.2$ km s⁻¹ |
| Ratio | — | $v_e/V_0 = \sqrt{2}$ |
| Orbit shape | Closed circle/ellipse | Open parabola or hyperbola |

| Aspect | g Above Surface | g Below Surface |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Formula | $g(h) \approx g_0(1-2h/R_E)$ | $g(d) = g_0(1-d/R_E)$ |
| Variation | Decreases as $1/(R_E+h)^2$ | Decreases linearly with d |
| At extremity | $g \to 0$ as $r \to \infty$ | $g = 0$ at Earth's centre |
| Rate for small displacement | Factor of $2h/R_E$ | Factor of $d/R_E$ |

## Common Misconceptions Clarified

| Misconception | Correct Understanding |
|:---|:---|
| Astronauts in space are weightless because there is no gravity | Gravity at orbital altitude is ~90% of surface value; weightlessness is due to free fall — both astronaut and spacecraft fall together |
| Escape speed depends on mass or direction of the projectile | Escape speed is independent of both; it depends only on the planet's mass and radius (and launch height) |
| A satellite in higher orbit moves faster | The opposite — higher orbit means lower orbital speed ($V \propto 1/\sqrt{r}$) |
| Gravitational PE is zero at Earth's surface | By convention, GPE = 0 at infinity; GPE at any finite distance is negative |
| Supplying energy to a satellite speeds it up | Supplying energy to raise orbit → satellite moves to higher orbit with LOWER speed (less KE, more PE) |
| Moon causes weaker tides than Sun because Moon is smaller | Tidal force depends on gradient ($dF/dr \propto 1/r^3$); Moon is much closer → steeper gradient → stronger tides |
| $g = 0$ everywhere inside Earth | $g = 0$ only at Earth's centre; it varies linearly from $g_0$ at surface to 0 at centre |
| Kepler's 2nd law is unique to gravitation | Law of areas holds for ANY central force; it is equivalent to conservation of angular momentum |
